<p><i><b>Wild Tales</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i lang="es"><b>Relatos salvajes</b></i>) is a 2014 <a href="/wiki/Black_comedy" title="Black comedy">black comedy</a> <a href="/wiki/Anthology_film" title="Anthology film">anthology film</a> composed of six standalone shorts, all written and directed by <a href="/wiki/Dami%C3%A1n_Szifron" title="Damián Szifron">Damián Szifron</a>, united by a common theme of violence and vengeance.
</p><p>It stars an <a href="/wiki/Ensemble_cast" title="Ensemble cast">ensemble cast</a> consisting of <a href="/wiki/Ricardo_Dar%C3%ADn" title="Ricardo Darín">Ricardo Darín</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Mart%C3%ADnez_(actor)" title="Oscar Martínez (actor)">Oscar Martínez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_Sbaraglia" title="Leonardo Sbaraglia">Leonardo Sbaraglia</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%89rica_Rivas" title="Érica Rivas">Érica Rivas</a>, Rita Cortese, <a href="/wiki/Julieta_Zylberberg" title="Julieta Zylberberg">Julieta Zylberberg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dar%C3%ADo_Grandinetti" title="Darío Grandinetti">Darío Grandinetti</a>, and was co-produced by <a href="/wiki/Agust%C3%ADn_Almod%C3%B3var" title="Agustín Almodóvar">Agustín Almodóvar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Almod%C3%B3var" title="Pedro Almodóvar">Pedro Almodóvar</a>. The film's <a href="/wiki/Film_score" title="Film score">musical score</a> was composed by <a href="/wiki/Gustavo_Santaolalla" title="Gustavo Santaolalla">Gustavo Santaolalla</a>.
</p><p>The film received critical acclaim, particularly in South America where it won many accolades, as well as the <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Film_Not_in_the_English_Language" title="BAFTA Award for Best Film Not in the English Language">BAFTA Award for Best Film Not in the English Language</a> in the United Kingdom and the <a href="/wiki/Goya_Award_for_Best_Spanish_Language_Foreign_Film" class="mw-redirect" title="Goya Award for Best Spanish Language Foreign Film">Goya Award for Best Spanish Language Foreign Film</a> in Spain. Additionally, it was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Foreign_Language_Film" class="mw-redirect" title="Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film">Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film</a> at the <a href="/wiki/87th_Academy_Awards" title="87th Academy Awards">87th Academy Awards</a>, and for the <a href="/wiki/Palme_d%27Or" title="Palme d&#39;Or">Palme d'Or</a> at the <a href="/wiki/2014_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2014 Cannes Film Festival">2014 Cannes Film Festival</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_Tales_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film is composed of six short segments: "Pasternak", "Las ratas" ("The Rats"), "El más fuerte" ("The Strongest"), "Bombita" ("Little Bomb"), "La propuesta" ("The Proposal"), and "Hasta que la muerte nos separe" ("Till Death Do Us Part"). In the American <a href="/wiki/Language_localisation" title="Language localisation">localisation</a>, the third and the fifth had their names changed to "Road to Hell" and "The Bill".
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Pasternak.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Pasternak&quot;">"Pasternak"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_Tales_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: &quot;Pasternak&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two passengers on an airplane start a conversation and discover they both know a man named Pasternak: the woman (María Marull) is his ex-girlfriend, and the man (<a href="/wiki/Dar%C3%ADo_Grandinetti" title="Darío Grandinetti">Darío Grandinetti</a>) is a <a href="/wiki/Music_journalism" title="Music journalism">music critic</a> who savagely reviewed his work. Several other passengers speak up and claim to know Pasternak as well. In fact, everyone on the flight is somehow connected to Pasternak, and all of their relationships with him ended negatively. A flight attendant reveals that Pasternak is the plane's cabin chief and has locked himself inside the cockpit. Amidst the panic, Pasternak crashes the plane into his parents' house.
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Las_ratas.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Las_ratas&quot;">"Las ratas"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_Tales_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: &quot;Las ratas&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Loan_shark" title="Loan shark">loan shark</a> (César Bordón) is the only customer at a small restaurant by a highway. He treats the waitress (<a href="/wiki/Julieta_Zylberberg" title="Julieta Zylberberg">Julieta Zylberberg</a>) very poorly when ordering his food. She recognizes him as the man who, years before, ruined her family and caused her father's untimely death. The cook (Rita Cortese), offers to mix <a href="/wiki/Rat_poison" class="mw-redirect" title="Rat poison">rat poison</a> into the man's food. The waitress refuses the cook's offer, but unbeknownst to her the cook adds the poison anyway. When the waitress finds out, she does not take the food away from the man.
</p><p>The man's teenage son arrives and begins to share his father's meal. Feeling guilty that the boy might eat the poison, the waitress tries to take the plate away. The man attacks her after she throws food in his face, but the cook kills him with a chef's knife. The last scene depicts the son getting medical treatment from a responding ambulance while the waitress sits next to him. Meanwhile, the cook is arrested and driven away in a police car.
</p>
<h3><span id=".22El_m.C3.A1s_fuerte.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;El_más_fuerte&quot;">"El más fuerte"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_Tales_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: &quot;El más fuerte&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Diego (<a href="/wiki/Leonardo_Sbaraglia" title="Leonardo Sbaraglia">Leonardo Sbaraglia</a>) is driving through the desert and tries to overtake a slower, older car that consistently blocks his path. When he finally passes, he insults the other driver, Mario (Walter Donado). Further up the road, Diego gets a flat tire, while Mario catches up and takes his revenge. He parks his car in front of Diego's, smashes his windshield, and then defecates and urinates on it. Just as Mario is about to leave, Diego pushes him and his car into the river and drives off.
</p><p>Fearing retribution from Mario, Diego soon returns to run Mario down, but loses control and crashes into the river. Mario enters Diego's car through the trunk, and both men start to fight. Mario leaves Diego being strangled by a <a href="/wiki/Seat_belt" title="Seat belt">seat belt</a> around his neck.  He then rips off a piece of his shirt, lights it on fire and places it in the gas tank in an attempt to incinerate the car, but Diego grabs him and prevents him from escaping. As the car explodes, the <a href="/wiki/Tow_truck" title="Tow truck">tow truck</a> driver (called by Diego) finally arrives. The police later discover the two charred bodies holding onto each other and mistake them for lovers who died in a crime of passion.
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Bombita.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Bombita&quot;">"Bombita"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_Tales_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: &quot;Bombita&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Simón Fischer (<a href="/wiki/Ricardo_Dar%C3%ADn" title="Ricardo Darín">Ricardo Darín</a>), a <a href="/wiki/Demolition" title="Demolition">demolition expert</a>, picks up a cake for his daughter's birthday party and discovers that his car has been towed. He goes to the towed-car lot and argues with an employee to no avail, insisting that there were no signs of a no-parking zone. He grudgingly pays the towing fee and misses his daughter's party. The next day, when Simón is refused a refund for the fee at the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Motor_Vehicles" title="Department of Motor Vehicles">DMV</a>, he attacks the glass partition and is arrested. The story makes the news and Fischer's company fires him. His wife (<a href="/wiki/Nancy_Dupl%C3%A1a" title="Nancy Dupláa">Nancy Dupláa</a>) seeks a divorce and sole custody of their daughter.
</p><p>After Fischer applies unsuccessfully for another job, he discovers that his car has been towed again. He retrieves the car and packs it with explosives in a tow zone. After it is towed again, he detonates the explosives, destroying the towing office with no casualties. Fischer is imprisoned and becomes a local hero, with calls on <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a> for his release. His wife and daughter visit him in prison for his birthday, bringing him a cake in the form of a tow truck.
</p>
<h3><span id=".22La_propuesta.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;La_propuesta&quot;">"La propuesta"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_Tales_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: &quot;La propuesta&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A teenager, in his father's car, arrives home after committing a <a href="/wiki/Hit_and_run" title="Hit and run">hit-and-run</a> on a pregnant woman. The woman and her child are reported dead on the local news, and her husband swears vengeance. The driver's parents (<a href="/wiki/Oscar_Mart%C3%ADnez_(actor)" title="Oscar Martínez (actor)">Oscar Martínez</a> and María Onetto) form a plan with his lawyer (<a href="/wiki/Osmar_N%C3%BA%C3%B1ez" title="Osmar Núñez">Osmar Núñez</a>) to have their groundskeeper José (Germán de Silva) take the blame for half a million dollars. However, the local prosecutor (Diego Velázquez) sees through the scheme because the car's mirrors were not adjusted for the caretaker's line of sight.
</p><p>The lawyer negotiates to include the prosecutor in the deal for more money, the caretaker asks for an apartment along with his money, and the prosecutor asks for an additional payment to pay off the police. However, the guilty son says that he wants to confess to the crowd gathered outside the house. Frustrated, the father calls off the deal, telling his son to confess. The lawyer renegotiates and the father agrees on a lower price. As José is taken away by the police, the dead woman's husband strikes him repeatedly on the head with a hammer.
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Hasta_que_la_muerte_nos_separe.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Hasta_que_la_muerte_nos_separe&quot;">"Hasta que la muerte nos separe"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_Tales_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: &quot;Hasta que la muerte nos separe&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At a <a href="/wiki/Jewish_wedding" title="Jewish wedding">Jewish wedding</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> party, the bride, Romina (<a href="/wiki/%C3%89rica_Rivas" title="Érica Rivas">Érica Rivas</a>), discovers that her groom, Ariel (Diego Gentile), has cheated on her with one of the guests. She confronts him as they dance in front of everyone, and runs off in distress to the roof, where a kitchen worker comforts her. Ariel discovers Romina having sex with the worker. She vindictively announces to him that she will sleep with every man who shows her interest, and take him for all he is worth if he tries to divorce her, or when he dies. They return to the party and continue the festivities.
</p><p>Romina pulls the woman Ariel slept with onto the dance floor, spins her around, and slams her into a mirror. She insists that the photographer film Ariel and his mother weeping, declaring that she will show it at a future wedding. Ariel's mother attacks Romina but is pulled off by her husband and Romina's father; Romina collapses out of sheer emotion. Ariel approaches Romina and extends a hand. They dance, kiss, and begin to have sex by the cake as the guests leave.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_Tales_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2007, director Damián Szifron had a break from directing and producing TV series and focused on writing.<sup id="cite_ref-Bathtub_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bathtub-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Alongside working on three major projects at the same time—a science fiction film series, a western and a love story—Szifron was writing short stories just to "let off steam", and eventually realized they were related.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Carpetbagger_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpetbagger-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Filmmaker_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Filmmaker-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> There were initially twelve tales, out of which he chose the "wilder" ones.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The second, third, fourth and sixth segments were partially based on real-life situations Szifron went through;<sup id="cite_ref-Carpetbagger_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpetbagger-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Collider_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collider-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> the second was written in a road immediately after he had an argument with an <a href="/wiki/Audi" title="Audi">Audi</a> driver.<sup id="cite_ref-Collider_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collider-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> At first, all stories were written as independent ones, and each of them could have been made into a film. However, Szifron thought that grouped they would have more impact so he decided to "reduce the conflicts to its minimum and find their climaxes."<sup id="cite_ref-losAndes2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-losAndes2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Often described as a black humor film, Szifron stressed the stories were not planned as comedies but rather as a thriller or as a drama depending on the part;<sup id="cite_ref-Filmmaker_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Filmmaker-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> in fact "they begin as dramas. The humor is a consequence of what these characters feel in a very dramatic situation."<sup id="cite_ref-Wallenberg_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wallenberg-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Ultimately, he thought neither comedy nor drama were appropriate labels, and considered that "catastrophe movie" is a good term for it.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>All stories are very different; production designer Clara Notari said "They have their own visual identity, as if each were a different movie, with its own spatial dimensions, colors, style, textures and set decoration".<sup id="cite_ref-Bathtub_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bathtub-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Despite this, Szifron stressed they "are vital organs of the same body" that sustain the film<sup id="cite_ref-NYMaking_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYMaking-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and "together [they] are more robust and make a larger universe".<sup id="cite_ref-losAndes2_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-losAndes2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Because of this desire, Szifron scrapped an early pre-production idea of having each episode done in different cinematographic styles—the fifth would be a black-and-white take; the second would be shot with anamorphic lenses and 35mm, and the last with social event video cameras.<sup id="cite_ref-mm_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mm-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Anyway, the director said the film's strength is not in the connection the accounts have. He asserted that "they are independent stories, with separate independent characters and conflicts".<sup id="cite_ref-losAndes2_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-losAndes2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Szifron explained that this was because he was inspired by <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept</a> and jazz albums, and by the circus. For the albums, the tracks, although a unity, have their "own identity"; during a circus spectacle there are different acts that have value for different reasons but they are one whole.<sup id="cite_ref-Carpetbagger_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpetbagger-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Szifron was also influenced by television anthology series <i><a href="/wiki/Amazing_Stories_(1985_TV_series)" title="Amazing Stories (1985 TV series)">Amazing Stories</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-BFI_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BFI-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock_Presents" title="Alfred Hitchcock Presents">Alfred Hitchcock Presents</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Twilight_Zone" title="The Twilight Zone">The Twilight Zone</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Wallenberg_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wallenberg-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> as well as film <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Stories" title="New York Stories">New York Stories</a></i>, and book <i><a href="/wiki/Nine_Stories_(Salinger)" title="Nine Stories (Salinger)">Nine Stories</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-BFI_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BFI-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The film was a co-production between the Argentine Kramer &amp; Sigman Films, <a href="/wiki/Telefe" title="Telefe">Telefe Productions</a> and Corner Contenidos and the Spanish company <a href="/wiki/El_Deseo" title="El Deseo">El Deseo</a>, owned by <a href="/wiki/Agust%C3%ADn_Almod%C3%B3var" title="Agustín Almodóvar">Agustín</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Almod%C3%B3var" title="Pedro Almodóvar">Pedro Almodóvar</a><sup id="cite_ref-losAndes_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-losAndes-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Argentina_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Argentina-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cadena3_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cadena3-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>—the Almodóvars were first interested to work with Szifron after watching his 2005 film <i><a href="/wiki/On_Probation_(2005_film)" title="On Probation (2005 film)">On Probation</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bathtub_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bathtub-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> With a budget of $4 million—70% from Argentina and 30% from Spain, <i>Wild Tales</i> had an eight-week shoot between April and May 2013,<sup id="cite_ref-Bathtub_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bathtub-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and had locations in cities of <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires_Province" title="Buenos Aires Province">Buenos Aires</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salta_Province" title="Salta Province">Salta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jujuy_Province" title="Jujuy Province">Jujuy</a> provinces.<sup id="cite_ref-losAndes2_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-losAndes2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-losAndes_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-losAndes-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The third act was specifically filmed on the route that connects <a href="/wiki/Salta" title="Salta">Salta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cafayate" title="Cafayate">Cafayate</a><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> because, as Szifron declared, "the script required a desolate route with stunning scenery and degrees of sobriety" to have a contrast between the violence of the fight and the beauty of the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In the end of the filming, there was material for "three different movies with different takes" so Szifron went through a six- or seven-month process of editing the film, which was done in his house.<sup id="cite_ref-Filmmaker_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Filmmaker-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Collider_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collider-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Each one of the main cast members were considered by Szifron to be main-role actors that are rarely seen in the same film.<sup id="cite_ref-Collider_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collider-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The director said it was possible to assemble these "very important actors" because they appreciated the screenplays, and because the shooting only took ten days for each short.<sup id="cite_ref-Collider_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collider-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Darín and Martínez were actors Szifron already wanted to work with and as such they were given the opportunity to choose the role they wanted.<sup id="cite_ref-Carpetbagger_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpetbagger-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">Themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_Tales_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:33%;&#32;;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r887775652">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%;max-width:100%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">We are all conscious that this system is not organised in our benefit. We behave like good citizens, we pay our taxes and we are law abiding citizens and buy the products that they sell to us but at the same time we all feel uncomfortable, as the ceiling is low or we didn’t have oxygen. This creates a great pressure that reflects in our love life or family life. We suffer this lack of time, this extreme difficulty to make money and do so many things that we are not interested in. Facing that reality, a lot of people get depressed and some others explode. This is a film about those who explode, those who cross the limit and reveal how really things work.</div>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style="">—Damián Szifron<sup id="cite_ref-ScreenDaily_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ScreenDaily-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>Despite the clear common theme of violence and vengeance,<sup id="cite_ref-weissberg_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weissberg-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bradshaw_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bradshaw-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> what connects the accounts, according to the director, is "the fuzzy boundary that separates civilization from barbarism, the vertigo of losing your temper, and the undeniable pleasure of losing control".<sup id="cite_ref-losAndes_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-losAndes-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> This is explored through the concept that human beings have animalistic features. Szifron considers the main difference between humans and animals is the capacity one has to restrain oneself, as opposed to animals who are guided by their instincts. Humans "have a fight or flee mechanism, but it comes with a very high cost. Most of us live with the frustration of having to repress oneself, but some people explode. This is a movie about those who explode". It deals with "daily life" aspects and "is a movie about the desire for freedom, and how this lack of freedom, and the rage and anguish it produces, can cause us to run off the rails."<sup id="cite_ref-NYMaking_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYMaking-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The main issue, according to Szifron, "is the pleasure of reacting, the pleasure of reacting toward injustice."<sup id="cite_ref-Wallenberg_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wallenberg-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The film is said to feature a "political anger"<sup id="cite_ref-BFI_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BFI-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and "political subversiveness,"<sup id="cite_ref-wilkins_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wilkins-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> and, according to <i><a href="/wiki/La_Capital" title="La Capital">La Capital</a></i>, it has sparked debates about its "sociological and political side."<sup id="cite_ref-capital_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-capital-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> As such, the newspaper promoted a debate about the film with <a href="/wiki/Social_security" title="Social security">social security</a> specialist Martín Appiolaza and sociologist Daniel Cholakian. Appiolaza said the film shows both <a href="/wiki/Structural_violence" title="Structural violence">structural violence</a> and specific violence towards school and work and among couples, as well as different types of discrimination. He concluded, "the theme of the film is the inequality that arouses violent reactions".<sup id="cite_ref-capital_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-capital-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In contrast, Cholakian stated it depicted a particular form of violence; the violence that involves upper-middle-class urban people. He affirmed that in reality the main victims of social violence are the poorest people, "So the film is light years from reflecting the universality of that theme".<sup id="cite_ref-capital_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-capital-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Cholakian went further, commenting he refused to discuss violence from <i>Wild Tales</i> and dared to think that Szifron would agree with him.<sup id="cite_ref-capital_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-capital-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> However, Szifron believed it had a universal range "in a world where power is concentrated in the hands of a small group of wealthy and powerful individuals."<sup id="cite_ref-Wallenberg_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wallenberg-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several reviewers interpreted the film as a critique of "21st-century Argentinean daily life"<sup id="cite_ref-nashawaty_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nashawaty-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> or "a backdrop of 20th century Argentine oppression".<sup id="cite_ref-newsday_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newsday-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> However, Szifron said it could be set "in any other country and in any other period of time".<sup id="cite_ref-Wallenberg_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wallenberg-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> He declared the central theme to be universal: "man versus a system that's designed against him, not to facilitate life, but to take things out of you."<sup id="cite_ref-Wallenberg_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wallenberg-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> As such, it criticises several problems and "frustrations of contemporary life",<sup id="cite_ref-rooney_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rooney-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> including government and corporate corruption, bureaucratic malfeasance,<sup id="cite_ref-Wallenberg_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wallenberg-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> economic and social inequality,<sup id="cite_ref-rooney_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rooney-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> abuse of power,<sup id="cite_ref-mm_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mm-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> emotional and physical abuse,<sup id="cite_ref-BFI_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BFI-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> class and <a href="/wiki/Gender_bias" class="mw-redirect" title="Gender bias">gender bias</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-kohn_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kohn-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> social exigence of marriage,<sup id="cite_ref-ScreenDaily_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ScreenDaily-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Macho" class="mw-redirect" title="Macho">macho</a> culture, and need for revenge.<sup id="cite_ref-horton_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-horton-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> It deals with money, power and <a href="/wiki/Elitism" title="Elitism">elitism</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and depicts people as selfish, disloyal, and <a href="/wiki/Economic_materialism" title="Economic materialism">materialistic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BFI_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BFI-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In such a "<a href="/wiki/Social_Darwinian" class="mw-redirect" title="Social Darwinian">social Darwinian</a> world",<sup id="cite_ref-dargis_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dargis-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> the acts of vengeance are usually motivated by class or economic conflicts,<sup id="cite_ref-solomon_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-solomon-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and underlying them is a desire to break free from what Szifron refers to as the "capitalist cage".<sup id="cite_ref-BFI_15-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BFI-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Claudia Puig of <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> said that it explores "the dark side of humanity and the dehumanization of society",<sup id="cite_ref-puig_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-puig-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> while Michael O'Sullivan of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> felt it was "a sharply observed case study in human nature."<sup id="cite_ref-sullivan_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sullivan-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Eric Kohn of <a href="/wiki/IndieWire" title="IndieWire">IndieWire</a> affirmed that the last segment alters the overall meaning of the film: "The bizarrely touching conclusion is a cynical take on the ups and downs of a relationship, hinting at the idea that even a mad world divided against itself thrives on the need for companionship."<sup id="cite_ref-kohn_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kohn-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> On the other hand, Ty Burr from <i><a href="/wiki/Boston_Globe" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Globe">Boston Globe</a></i> concluded, "there is no lasting meaning, other than that people are funny, nasty animals when pushed to their limits."<sup id="cite_ref-burr_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-burr-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_and_reception">Release and reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_Tales_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Release and reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_Tales_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Wild Tales</i> was critically acclaimed;<sup id="cite_ref-mm_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mm-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> in Argentina, its appraisal was described by <i><a href="/wiki/Clar%C3%ADn_(Argentine_newspaper)" title="Clarín (Argentine newspaper)">Clarín</a></i> as "a phenomenal reception",<sup id="cite_ref-clarin_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clarin-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> and English-speaking reviewers were also favourable.<sup id="cite_ref-rt_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rt-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mc_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mc-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, based on 152 reviews, <i>Wild Tales</i> holds a 94% "fresh" rating, with an average score of 8/10, and with the critical consensus being: "Wickedly hilarious and delightfully deranged, <i>Wild Tales</i> is a subversive satire that doubles as a uniformly entertaining anthology film".<sup id="cite_ref-rt_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rt-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> reports an average score of 77, based on 33 reviews, indicating "generally favorable reviews".<sup id="cite_ref-mc_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mc-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> commentator Richard Corliss compared the film to an <a href="/wiki/Ambrose_Bierce" title="Ambrose Bierce">Ambrose Bierce</a> or <a href="/wiki/Roald_Dahl" title="Roald Dahl">Roald Dahl</a> story, calling it "the year's most fearlessly funny film", and naming it the ninth best film of 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Burr stated it has a "masterful sense of cinematic storytelling",<sup id="cite_ref-burr_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-burr-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> while O'Sullivan affirmed "Szifron handles the tone and presentation masterfully".<sup id="cite_ref-sullivan_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sullivan-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Ariston Anderson from <i><a href="/wiki/Filmmaker_(magazine)" title="Filmmaker (magazine)">Filmmaker</a></i> commented, "There is truly never a dull moment ... <i>Wild Tales</i> is a laugh-out-loud riot from start to finish",<sup id="cite_ref-Filmmaker_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Filmmaker-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and Mar Diestro-Dópido of <i><a href="/wiki/Sight_%26_Sound" title="Sight &amp; Sound">Sight &amp; Sound</a></i> said, "Each morsel of well-rounded, perfectly structured storytelling becomes part of a coherent, exuberant whole".<sup id="cite_ref-BFI_15-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BFI-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <i>Clarín</i> film critic, Pablo O. Scholz, said "The film pulls us in from the start until the end". Although he commented each story tone is different, Scholz stated the "tension ... never decays, and keeps the viewer with a knot in the stomatch for two hours".<sup id="cite_ref-clarin_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clarin-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> In opposition, Charles Solomon of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> was critical of its pace: "Revenge may be a dish best served cold, but Argentine writer-director Damián Szifron allows it to sit until it congeals in the dreary six-part anthology <i>Wild Tales</i>."<sup id="cite_ref-solomon_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-solomon-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Robert Horton from <i><a href="/wiki/Seattle_Weekly" title="Seattle Weekly">Seattle Weekly</a></i> further criticized it for relying on twists, "a technique that doesn't quite disguise how in-your-face the lessons are", and ultimately dubbed the film "a scattering of gotchas".<sup id="cite_ref-horton_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-horton-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The humor was called "a subversive [one] that manages to be both psychologically astute and all-out outrageous" by Jay Weissberg of <i>Variety</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-weissberg_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weissberg-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Diestro-Dópido said "The humour of <i>Wild Tales</i> is pitch-black, its irony razor-sharp, its sarcasm painfully perverse and its unpredictability ludicrous, violent but also bitingly funny".<sup id="cite_ref-BFI_15-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BFI-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Manohla Dargis from <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> compared its use of humor and coincidence to <a href="/wiki/O._Henry" title="O. Henry">O. Henry</a>'s work,<sup id="cite_ref-dargis_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dargis-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> as did Chris Nashawaty of <i>Entertainment Weekly</i> because of its "ironic twist endings".<sup id="cite_ref-nashawaty_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nashawaty-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The cast was deemed consistent through the episodes by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s David Rooney,<sup id="cite_ref-rooney_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rooney-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> and said to be "superb" by Diestro-Dópido.<sup id="cite_ref-BFI_15-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BFI-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Érica Rivas received particular appraisal for her "star-making performance", as dubbed by Bruce Ingram of <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Sun-Times" title="Chicago Sun-Times">Chicago Sun-Times</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ingram_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ingram-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> While David Edelstein of <a href="/wiki/Vulture.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Vulture.com">Vulture.com</a> said the cast is "incredibly credible given the characters' outlandish behavior,"<sup id="cite_ref-edelstein_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edelstein-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Horton was critical of the characterization, stating characters have "illogical behavior", mostly in the last segment.<sup id="cite_ref-horton_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-horton-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, Richard Brody of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i> said it "offers little in the way of context or characterization" and then "characters behave so rudely and crudely, there's no reason to care about what happens to them."<sup id="cite_ref-brody_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brody-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> On the other hand, O'Sullivan declared, "The protagonists are exaggerated without being caricatures",<sup id="cite_ref-sullivan_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sullivan-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> and Jordan Hoffman of the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Daily_News" title="New York Daily News">New York Daily News</a></i> affirmed, "All are funny because all feel true".<sup id="cite_ref-hoffman_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoffman-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Puig, Steven Rea of <a href="/wiki/Philly" class="mw-redirect" title="Philly">Philly</a>.com and Diestro-Dópido also felt the characters to be believable and relatable because most viewers would feel familiar with the situations or frustrations the characters go through.<sup id="cite_ref-BFI_15-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BFI-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-puig_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-puig-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rea_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rea-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>"Visuals are flawless", stated Weissberg, who praised both its special effects and its cinematography by Javier Julia, who has "a lean sense of irony that adds to the general pleasure".<sup id="cite_ref-weissberg_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weissberg-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The reason it is a "good-looking film ... crafted in high style" is, in Rooney's opinion, credited to "lots of eye-catching touches from production designer Clara Notari and unconventional camera angles from cinematographer Javier Julia."<sup id="cite_ref-rooney_28-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rooney-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> For Peter Howell of <i><a href="/wiki/Toronto_Star" title="Toronto Star">Toronto Star</a></i>, Julia "imparts a visual appreciation of the absurd that's somewhere between a <a href="/wiki/Looney_Tunes" title="Looney Tunes">Looney Tunes</a> cartoon and <a href="/wiki/Grand_Guignol" title="Grand Guignol">Grand Guignol</a> theatre."<sup id="cite_ref-howell_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-howell-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The music by Gustavo Santaollala was deemed "a terrific <a href="/wiki/Spaghetti_Western" title="Spaghetti Western">spaghetti Western</a>-flavored score" by Rooney.<sup id="cite_ref-rooney_28-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rooney-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Weissberg affirmed the soundtrack "fits the tone without pushing any wink-wink superiority".<sup id="cite_ref-weissberg_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weissberg-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Weissberg commented that while "the overall enjoyment rarely flags", "not all the episodes are equally successful".<sup id="cite_ref-weissberg_22-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weissberg-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> For example, Weissberg and O'Sullivan said "The Bill" felt displaced within the film because of its "darker tone",<sup id="cite_ref-weissberg_22-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weissberg-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and its "mood of bitter cynicism.<sup id="cite_ref-sullivan_35-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sullivan-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> According to Howell, "It lacks the manic love of the ridiculous that runs through other sketches",<sup id="cite_ref-howell_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-howell-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> and Puig affirmed it "borders on melodrama."<sup id="cite_ref-puig_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-puig-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Rooney further considered it and "Bombita" had "a more sober tone that lets the air out of the balloon of delirious mayhem created by the opening three episodes."<sup id="cite_ref-rooney_28-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rooney-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In fact, the first segment was said to be "a perfect starter" by Puig,<sup id="cite_ref-puig_34-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-puig-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and Horton said "the rest of this anthology feature doesn't live up to the wicked curtain-raiser".<sup id="cite_ref-horton_30-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-horton-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Also, Rea considered it to the best segment along with the last one.<sup id="cite_ref-rea_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rea-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> While Hoffman commented, "The final set piece is the most outrageous",<sup id="cite_ref-hoffman_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoffman-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Nashawaty deemed "The Bill" the best segment,<sup id="cite_ref-nashawaty_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nashawaty-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> and David Edelstein of Vulture.com considered "Bombita" "the purest of all the tales, the one that distills the mad-as-hell vigilante".<sup id="cite_ref-edelstein_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edelstein-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Nashawaty criticized "Road to Hell" because it "feel[s] like [a] cheeky one-joke setup in search of a second or third joke".<sup id="cite_ref-nashawaty_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nashawaty-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In opposition, Weissberg affirmed "Szifron's consummate skill at narration and setup, combined with inventive absurdity, makes it fresh and thoroughly entertaining."<sup id="cite_ref-weissberg_22-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weissberg-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Both in Europe and Latin America film critics dubbed it "Characters on the Verge of a Nervous Breakdown", alluding to Almodóvar's <i><a href="/wiki/Women_on_the_Verge_of_a_Nervous_Breakdown" title="Women on the Verge of a Nervous Breakdown">Women on the Verge of a Nervous Breakdown</a></i>, which Almodóvar himself considered a fair comparison.<sup id="cite_ref-NYMaking_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYMaking-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> For example, on Almodóvar influence, Rooney said "It's there in the off-kilter humor, in the stylish visuals and bold use of music, and in the affection for ordinary people pushed to extraordinary extremes."<sup id="cite_ref-rooney_28-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rooney-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Bob Mondello of <a href="/wiki/NPR" title="NPR">NPR</a> said the last segment "is weird, sexy and violent enough to make you think of the wild tales of director Pedro Almodóvar".<sup id="cite_ref-mondello_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mondello-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Other similarities were noted, including to <i><a href="/wiki/Pulp_Fiction" title="Pulp Fiction">Pulp Fiction</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Wallenberg_11-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wallenberg-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bradshaw_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bradshaw-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nashawaty_26-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nashawaty-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Duel_(1971_film)" title="Duel (1971 film)">Duel</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Wallenberg_11-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wallenberg-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bradshaw_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bradshaw-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Douglas" title="Michael Douglas">Michael Douglas</a>-featuring <i><a href="/wiki/Falling_Down" title="Falling Down">Falling Down</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Wallenberg_11-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wallenberg-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Emir_Kusturica" title="Emir Kusturica">Emir Kusturica</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bradshaw_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bradshaw-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> While "Pasternak" was often compared to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Twilight_Zone" title="The Twilight Zone">The Twilight Zone</a></i> because of its series of revelations,<sup id="cite_ref-horton_30-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-horton-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-puig_34-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-puig-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mondello_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mondello-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> the third segment was often compared to a cartoon, especifically to a <a href="/wiki/Road_Runner_and_Wile_E._Coyote" class="mw-redirect" title="Road Runner and Wile E. Coyote">Road Runner and Wile E. Coyote</a> cartoon,<sup id="cite_ref-dargis_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dargis-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-howell_46-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-howell-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> and was said to have <a href="/wiki/Slapstick" title="Slapstick">slapstick</a> touches<sup id="cite_ref-ingram_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ingram-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mondello_47-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mondello-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> and to be "Tarantino-esque".<sup id="cite_ref-puig_34-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-puig-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mondello_47-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mondello-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> It was also labeled "a cross between Steven Spielberg's <i>Duel</i> and a violent <a href="/wiki/Tom_and_Jerry" title="Tom and Jerry">Tom and Jerry</a> cartoon" by Nashawaty,<sup id="cite_ref-nashawaty_26-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nashawaty-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> and "a combination of <i><a href="/wiki/Deliverance" title="Deliverance">Deliverance</a></i> and an R-rated Wile E. Coyote cartoon" by Liam Lacey of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Globe_and_Mail" title="The Globe and Mail">The Globe and Mail</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-lacey_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lacey-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_impact">Cultural impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_Tales_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cultural impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The New York Times</i> reported that it became "a genuine social phenomenon" and that some characters gained a cult status. For example, it inspired "I am Bombita" to become a catchphrase similar to "<a href="/wiki/Going_postal" title="Going postal">going postal</a>" in the United States. Rivas, from the last story, said she has been stopped in the street and asked several times to say "Film this for me, Nestor!".<sup id="cite_ref-NYMaking_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYMaking-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the 2015 crash of <a href="/wiki/Germanwings_Flight_9525" title="Germanwings Flight 9525">Germanwings Flight 9525</a>, BFI and Curzon cinemas modified their home cinema listings of this film stating that there was a similarity between the fictional crash at the start of the film and the real Germanwings crash.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades_and_public_reception">Accolades and public reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_Tales_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Accolades and public reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film debuted on 17 May 2014 at the <a href="/wiki/2014_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2014 Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-reuters_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> where it was selected to compete for its main prize, the <a href="/wiki/Palme_d%27Or" title="Palme d&#39;Or">Palme d'Or</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Cannes2014_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cannes2014-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> and had a ten-minute standing ovation.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The film's <a href="/wiki/Viral_phenomenon" title="Viral phenomenon">popularity exploded</a>, according to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i>, becoming a fan favorite during its exhibitions at <a href="/wiki/Telluride_Film_Festival" title="Telluride Film Festival">Telluride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toronto_International_Film_Festival" title="Toronto International Film Festival">Toronto</a> film festivals.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> After its praised festival tour,<sup id="cite_ref-Argentina_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Argentina-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> it was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Foreign_Language_Film" class="mw-redirect" title="Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film">Best Foreign Language Film</a> at the <a href="/wiki/87th_Academy_Awards" title="87th Academy Awards">87th Academy Awards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Argentina_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Argentina-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nominees_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nominees-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Out of nine nominations at the <a href="/wiki/29th_Goya_Awards" title="29th Goya Awards">29th Goya Awards</a>, it won the Best Ibero-American Film.<sup id="cite_ref-goya_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goya-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> During the 2nd Platino Awards, it won eight out of ten, including Best Picture and Best Director.<sup id="cite_ref-platino_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-platino-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-platino2_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-platino2-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> The film also won the Audience Award for Best European Film at the <a href="/wiki/San_Sebasti%C3%A1n_International_Film_Festival" title="San Sebastián International Film Festival">San Sebastián International Film Festival</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-SanSebastián_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SanSebastián-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> and the award for the Best Non-English Language Film at the <a href="/wiki/69th_British_Academy_Film_Awards" title="69th British Academy Film Awards">BAFTA Awards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bafta_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bafta-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The film opened in Argentina on 21 August 2014,<sup id="cite_ref-BOM_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BOM-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and during its first weekend it set the country's record for an opening with 450,000 tickets (approximately $2.5 million).<sup id="cite_ref-Argentina_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Argentina-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> After 24 days, it has become the most-seen film of the year in Argentina with more than two million spectators in 275 cinemas.<sup id="cite_ref-cadena3_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cadena3-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> By September, it was estimated that it had sold more than 2.4 million tickets, making it the most-seen Argentine film of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> In the same month, it became the first domestic film to surpass 100 million <a href="/wiki/Argentine_peso" title="Argentine peso">pesos</a> ($12m) at the box office.<sup id="cite_ref-ScreenDaily_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ScreenDaily-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> It sold over 3.9 million tickets and grossed $17.5 million in Argentina.<sup id="cite_ref-BOM_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BOM-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> Exhibited in 33 other countries, it grossed $27,006,530 worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-BOM_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BOM-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><br />
